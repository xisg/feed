These examples represent four classes of “M-W squares”,
word squares that can be made from headwords in the 5th edition of the
Merriam-Webster Collegiate Dictionary, including the
appendices of boys' and girls' names. Inflected forms, such as
plurals, are excluded unless they literally appear in the
dictionary (typically for irregular or multiple inflected forms).
The classes, whose sizes are given above, are

7×7 squares

6×6 double squares (different words across and down)

5×5 double squares with extra words on the diagnoals

4×4 motley squares (double squares, all letters different)

The displayed 7×7 and 6×6 squares were pronounced
best in class by the nonpareil word gamester Dmitri Borgmann.
[A. Ross Eckler, Language on Vacation St. Martin (1995)]

Each class is exemplified by a square of the largest size
that meets the class criterion.
There are thousands of M-W squares smaller than 7×7,
but none larger.
Double word squares are far less common: The number of
n×n double squares is comparable to the
number of n+1×n+1 unrestricted squares.
[Chris Long,

Mathematics of square construction]
The biggest M-W double word squares are 6×6s.

The 5×5 is a rare find. Only two
out of the 529,286 double squares of this size bear words on both diagonals.
The two differ in just one letter—either
A or I in the middle of PR_NK.
Not surprisingly, these trophies found in the bottom of
a very deep barrel are flush with uncommon words:
:

ARRAStapestry
ARRISsharp edge where surfaces meet, as in moldings
HORAEGreek goddesses of seasons
HOURIcompanion maiden in Muslim paradise
PARRS(plural) young salmon or trout
PECKYmarked with fungal decay, spalted
PRINKprimp

Enumerating word squares was a big deal when
I first did it in the 1970s. The search for 7×7s
sopped up spare hours on a PDP-11 computer over more than
two months. Now a laptop can do the job in minutes.
A version of the C program for the search
is available here.
Much word square lore, including more squares of mine, can be found in

Word Ways, an open-access publication with a good search
facility.

NAGS breaks the rules. For the motley-square search, the M-W word list was padded with plurals and third-person singular verbs that don't literally appear in the dictionary.

Two more squares can be made by replacing single letters in
the middle square so that

BLOC/BRAG become FLOC/FRAG, or
