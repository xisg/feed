## Progress on a classic problem implicitly posed by Dijkstra

Edsger Dijkstra once proudly told me that his work on “Self-stabilizing systems in spite of distributed control” \[Communications of the ACM **17** (1974), 643–644\] might well turn out to be his most significant contribution to computer science.

In 1985, when I led Stanford's CS problem-solving seminar, I decided to look closely at his methods together with our first-year grad students, trying to see if we could improve them by using fewer states. (This was Problem 4 of five problems, recorded for posterity as part of the ["Aha" Sessions](https://www.youtube.com/playlist?list=PLABJEFgj0PWW9fQwb8JVDRaorXNin8VgC) and still viewable on YouTube.)

Well, we learned quite a bit, but we did *not* succeed in solving the problem. Indeed, of the fifty or so problems that the students and I tackled during the years I taught that seminar, this was the one that proved to be the toughest.

I'm pleased to announce that Keston Aquino-Michaels has now shed a great deal of light on the problem, finding exact solutions up to *n* = 9 processors and formulating a tantalizing conjecture for larger values of *n*. His work (see his [github site](https://github.com/no-way-labs/minimum-state-product)) is steeped in modern methods that made this breakthrough possible: State-of-the-art SMT solvers; Lean's Mathlib; frontier LLM's as research agents; and of course computer power that was unimaginable in 1985.

## Spider squishing, revitalized

One of my favorite examples of “recursive coroutines” appears in a paper that I wrote with Frank Ruskey and dedicated to Ole-Johan Dahl on his 70th birthday in 2001. \[It was published on pages 183–208 of Lecture Notes in Computer Science **2635** (2004) with the title “Efficient coroutine generation of constrained Gray sequences.”\] These coroutines solve a general problem with many important special cases. The official high-falutin' name for the problem is “generating the order ideals of an acyclic poset”; but informally it is called “spider squishing.” \[I spoke about it in my Christmas Lecture of 2001; unfortunately the videotape of that lecture was loaned to somebody who never returned it. Maybe I should talk about it again some time?\]

The program that I put online at the time, called [`SPIDERS`](https://www-cs-faculty.stanford.edu/~knuth/programs/spiders.w), was intended to represent an especially instructive way to learn nontrivial ideas about tree structures, recursion, and combinatorial generation. However, my Korean friend Soojin Nam has just reported that the original program contained a [serious bug](https://github.com/sjnam/spider/tree/main/bugreport)—which he discovered with the help of Claude!

Fortunately he also told me how to fix the problem, and I believe that [`SPIDERS`](https://www-cs-faculty.stanford.edu/~knuth/programs/spiders.w) now works correctly at last. If you like algorithmic challenges, and have nothing better to do, you might want to check it out.

(Please note that I'm emphatically *not* in favor of squishing actual spiders in the real world. Spiders are marvelous creatures! “Spider nudging” would be a much better informal name for this topic.)

## TAOCP update

The fourth “volume” of [The Art of Computer Programming](https://www-cs-faculty.stanford.edu/~knuth/taocp.html) deals with Combinatorial Algorithms, the area of computer science where nonobvious techniques have the most dramatic effects. I love it the most, because one good idea can often make a program run a million times faster. It's a huge, fascinating subject, and Part 1 (Volume 4A, 883 pages, now in its twenty-sixth printing) was published in 2011; Part 2 (Volume 4B, 714 pages, now in its fourth printing) was published at the close of 2022. The first 275 or so pages of Volume 4C have just appeared in print, as announced above, together with an index.

While preparing many of the new exercises in Volumes 4B and 4C, I spent a lot of time attempting to improve on expositions that I found in the literature. And in several noteworthy cases, nobody has yet pointed out any errors. It would be nice to believe that I actually got the details right on my first attempt; but that seems unlikely, because I had hundreds of chances to make mistakes. So I fear that the most probable hypothesis is that nobody has been sufficiently motivated to check the finer points out carefully as yet.

I still cling to a belief that such details are extremely instructive. ## Knight's tours are alive and well

… and living in Cleveland!\

Olin Hall at Case Western Reserve University, home of the Computer and Data Sciences department, has just celebrated the completion of a major renovation project. In the spring of 2024 I was asked for advice about special artwork that might be appropriate to include in the remodeled building. This was a thrilling prospect for me, because I've long been a fan of “Geek Art” — artwork that appeals to both halves of people's brains by being doubly beautiful, having both visual beauty and scientific beauty. (See Chapter 47 in my [Selected Papers on Fun and Games](https://www-cs-faculty.stanford.edu/~knuth/fg.html).) What better place for Geek Art than the walls of a thriving CS department?

Thus began an exciting collaboration with the CWRU design team, led by [Kathleen Barrie](https://loebfellowship.gsd.harvard.edu/fellows-alumni/fellows-search/kathleen-barrie/). We decided to focus on the intriguing patterns called *knight's tours* — the longest possible sequences of knight moves on a chessboard, or on similar boards of different sizes. These striking patterns are not only eye-catching, they also rank among the earliest achievements in combinatorial mathematics and graph theory, because they have a venerable history going back more than 1200 years to ancient Kashmir.

Many different species of knight's tours can now be seen on the walls of Olin Hall, near the elevators on floors 3, 4, and 8. Closeup views and details about their surprisingly subtle properties can be found [here](https://www-cs-faculty.stanford.edu/~knuth/knights.html). (All of these photographs are due to Jerry Birchfield of [Field Studio Photography](https://www.fieldstudio.photography).)
